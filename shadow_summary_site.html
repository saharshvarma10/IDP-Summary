The Impact of Poverty & Inequality on Health, Nutrition, and Child Growth

Shadow Squad — a student team (Saharsh, Dhakshana, Waqar, Ira) researched how poverty and inequality influence health, nutrition, and the growth of children. This summary website presents key findings, the SDG links (No Poverty, Zero Hunger, Reduced Inequalities), global examples, and our team reflections and action plan.

Scroll down or use the menu above to navigate sections. Designed as a concise summary for presentation and sharing.

Why we chose this topic

Poverty and inequality remain among the world’s largest challenges. Many people lack basic needs — food, clean water, healthcare and education — while unequal resource distribution deepens the divide. Our team chose this topic to understand those effects on children and families and to highlight solutions aligned with the SDGs.

No Poverty (SDG 1)

Goal: End extreme poverty for all by 2030.

Poverty reduces access to healthcare, increases disease burden, and limits opportunities for growth. Millions still live on just a few dollars a day, with Sub‑Saharan Africa most affected. Poverty in childhood increases risk of stunting, chronic illness and life‑long disadvantages.

Health: Limited healthcare access and higher mortality in poor communities.
Nutrition: Insufficient diets lead to stunting and delays in development.
Child Growth: Early disadvantages shape long‑term physical and cognitive outcomes.

Zero Hunger (SDG 2)

Goal: End hunger, achieve food security and improved nutrition, and promote sustainable agriculture.

Hunger undermines learning, health and productivity. Childhood hunger has lasting physical and psychological consequences. Recent setbacks from the pandemic, conflict, and climate shocks have increased food insecurity globally — millions remain undernourished.

Key challenges include rising food prices, disrupted supply chains, and weakened agricultural investment in fragile regions.

Reduced Inequality (SDG 10)

Goal: Reduce inequality within and among countries by improving social, economic and political inclusion.

Inequality concentrates wealth and opportunity with a few while many are left behind. This affects health and nutrition by limiting access to services for marginalized groups. Addressing inequality includes social protection, disability inclusion, and pro‑poor economic policies.

Global Perspectives & Case Examples

Different regions face distinct challenges: Sub‑Saharan Africa has high extreme poverty; South Sudan struggles with food insecurity; countries like India, China and Indonesia have shown notable poverty reduction. Canada, the USA and Australia demonstrate diverse national measures and ongoing gaps that require policy responses.

Sub‑Saharan Africa: High rates of extreme poverty and limited services.
South Sudan: Conflict and poor infrastructure worsen food insecurity.
Progress Examples: Large poverty reductions recorded in India and China in recent decades.

Computational Thinking — Applied to Poverty

Computational thinking breaks complex problems into smaller parts, finds patterns, and builds step‑by‑step solutions. Applied to poverty, it helps identify root causes (education, health, employment), prioritize interventions, model outcomes and design scalable programs such as skills training, targeted social protection, and data‑driven monitoring.
